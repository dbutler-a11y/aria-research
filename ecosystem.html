The Foundation Remains Open

All foundational research, hardware designs, AI models, and software interfaces developed by ARIA are released under permissive open-source licenses. This means:

  • Anyone can study our research and methodology
  • Anyone can build ARIA devices for personal or community use
  • Anyone can extend, modify, or improve upon our designs
  • Institutions can evaluate our approach without vendor lock-in
  • Researchers can reproduce and validate our findings

This openness is not incidentalâ€”it is essential. Assistive technology for vulnerable populations must be transparent, auditable, and trustworthy. Families and healthcare providers need confidence that the systems they deploy are safe, well-understood, and free from hidden agendas.

Coordination Layers for Sustainability

While the core remains open, certain coordination functions are structured to ensure ARIA does not stagnate or fragment:

Quality Certification

Voluntary certification standards ensure that community-built devices meet safety and performance benchmarks before deployment in care settings.

Governance Coordination

Community governance structures guide roadmap priorities, research directions, and resource allocation through transparent, participatory processes.

Funding Stewardship

Donated funds are allocated through accountable, community-informed processes with public reporting on usage and outcomes.

These coordination layers exist to serve the open coreâ€”not to capture value from it. They ensure that ARIA remains a coherent, high-quality initiative rather than a fragmented collection of incompatible forks.

A Tokenized Participation Layer

Coordinating a growing ecosystem through recognition, governance, and accessâ€”not ownership.

As ARIA grows beyond a single research project into a global ecosystem of researchers, builders, caregivers, and institutions, we need mechanisms to coordinate participation, recognize contribution, and enable collective decision-making.

ARIA Participation Tokens serve this coordination function. They are issued to recognize meaningful contributions to the ecosystem and enable participation in its governance.

Tokens Are Issued to Recognize:

  • Donations â€” Financial contributions that fund research and development
  • Research contributions â€” Published findings, validated datasets, clinical insights
  • Development work â€” Code, hardware designs, documentation, translations
  • Community coordination â€” Organizing pilots, supporting builders, facilitating governance

Tokens Enable:

  • Governance participation in ecosystem decisions
  • Voting on roadmap priorities and research directions
  • Access to early pilots, datasets, and development previews
  • Proposal submission for funding allocation
  • Recognition within the contributor community

Tokens Do Not:

  • Represent ownership in any entity
  • Promise or imply financial return
  • Provide dividends, revenue shares, or profit claims
  • Function as investment instruments
  • Guarantee any future value

They are governance and recognition instrumentsâ€”tools for community stewardship, not vehicles for investment. Anyone approaching ARIA tokens with expectation of financial return has fundamentally misunderstood their purpose.

Transparency, Ethics, and Accountability

ARIA is designed to earn trust slowly and keep it permanently.

Working with vulnerable populationsâ€”elderly individuals, those with neurodegenerative conditions, their families and caregiversâ€”demands the highest ethical standards. Trust, once broken, cannot be rebuilt. We commit to practices that earn and maintain that trust.
